What is GAPandPower?

GAPandPower was born from a simple idea: running data should be insightful, not cluttered. While your watch can show you dozens of metrics, the most important ones are often lost on a crowded screen. This data field focuses on the four key metrics that give you the best understanding of your performance in real-time: Power, Grade Adjusted Pace (GAP), 3-Min Average Power, and the new Environmental Effect.

It works on almost any Garmin watch, new or old, without needing an expensive foot pod. Just install it, configure your weight, and run!

The Core Metrics Explained

Running Power (Watts)

Power is the ultimate measure of your output. Unlike pace, which is affected by hills, or heart rate, which lags behind your effort, power tells you exactly how hard you're working, right now.

Grade Adjusted Pace (GAP)

Ever wonder what your pace would be on flat ground while you're climbing a steep hill? That's GAP. It estimates your equivalent flat-ground pace, allowing you to maintain a consistent effort.

3-Min Avg Power

This rolling average smooths out fluctuations and gives you a stable target to hold. It's an excellent proxy for your short-term sustainable effort and helps you avoid going out too hard.

Environmental Effect (%)

This unique metric quantifies the combined effect of hills and wind as a percentage of your effort. A positive value means you're fighting the environment; a negative value means it's helping you.

How Power is Calculated: The Science

The power number on your watch is a sophisticated calculation based on a well-established physics model. It is the sum of three distinct components, each representing the work you do to overcome a specific force.

Total Power = Physiological + Aerodynamic + Climbing

1. Physiological Power (Pr)

This is the foundational power required to propel your body forward on a perfectly flat, windless surface. It's the basic metabolic cost of the running motion itself.

Pr = C * weight * speed
  • C: A running economy coefficient (0.98), representing the energy needed to move 1kg of mass over 1km.
  • weight: Your body mass in kilograms.
  • speed: Your current running speed in meters per second.

Why Your `weight` Setting is CRITICAL

This power component is directly proportional to your weight. An inaccurate weight setting is the single biggest source of error in your power data. Setting it correctly is essential.

2. Aerodynamic Power (Pa)

This is the power you expend to overcome air resistance. You are constantly pushing a column of air out of your way as you run.

Pa = 0.5 * Ro * CdA * relativeWindSpeedÂ³
  • Ro: The density of the air (constant).
  • CdA: Your coefficient of drag area (constant).
  • relativeWindSpeed: Your speed relative to the air (your speed + wind effect).

Why the `wind` Setting is So Important

Power needed to fight air resistance increases with the CUBE of relative wind speed. A light headwind can easily double this part of your power cost. If you don't set the wind on a windy day, your power will be significantly underestimated.

3. Climbing Power (Pc)

This is the power required to lift your body mass against gravity when running uphill. When running downhill, this value becomes negative, as gravity helps you.

Pc = (slope/100) * weight * G * speed * Nu
  • slope: The steepness of the terrain from GPS.
  • weight: Your body mass in kilograms.
  • G: The acceleration due to gravity (9.81 m/sÂ²).
  • Nu: A biomechanical efficiency factor that adjusts for how your body's efficiency changes on different grades.
